# Aggregator Pitch Template

Apply per-target profile adjustments. This is the structural skeleton; tone shifts across curators (Lenny = tactical warmth; Kai Brach = essayistic restraint; Packy = thesis-upgrade DM).

## Email pitch structure (150–250 words)

**Subject line:** One concrete noun from the essay + one verb describing what the essay argues. No "[New Essay]" brackets, no "Featured on briantighe.design" self-reference, no questions.

Good: `Prototype-led positioning — replacing the deck with a working artifact`
Bad: `[NEW POST] Why positioning decks are dead`

**Paragraph 1 — Personal (1–2 sentences).** Reference a specific recent piece or idea from the curator's own work. Show you actually read it. Avoid "love your newsletter" / "big fan" boilerplate.

**Paragraph 2 — The essay, concisely (3–4 sentences).** State the framework by its exact capitalized name. Include the receipt if the essay has one. Make the argument legible in the paragraph itself — do not require the curator to click through to understand the claim.

**Paragraph 3 — Positioning (1 sentence).** One line on Brian: operator-researcher, Principal Product Designer at Yahoo Mail, establishing the "design engineering for growth" category. Never "thought leader," "content creator," or "personal brand."

**Paragraph 4 — Ask (1 sentence).** Frame as an offer: "If it fits your editorial arc on X, I would be grateful for consideration." Never "please feature," never "would mean the world."

**Essay URL** on its own line, blank line above and below.

**Sign-off:** `— Brian` (simple, never "Best," / "Cheers," / "Thanks in advance," / marketing sign-offs).

## DM pitch structure (80–120 words)

**Opener:** Specific reference to a thing the curator just wrote or said. No "hey loved your X."

**Middle:** Two sentences stating the framework and its receipt. Write it so a curator who replies "interesting, tell me more" sees that the essay answers the natural follow-up.

**Close:** Link to the essay. Offer no ask. If they want to feature it, they will; if not, the conversation can continue without awkwardness.

## Submission form structure

If the aggregator uses a web form with labeled fields:

- **Title:** Essay title verbatim from briantighe.design
- **URL:** Canonical essay URL
- **Why this fits your publication:** 40–60 words following the email paragraph 2 structure
- **Your name:** Brian Tighe
- **Your role:** Principal Product Designer, Yahoo Mail
- **Category / topic:** Match the form's available options to the essay's closest fit

## Common adjustments per surface

- **Lenny's Newsletter:** Lead with the receipt (specific metric) in paragraph 2. Lenny filters on operator specificity.
- **Dense Discovery:** Drop the receipt if it reads as marketing; Kai values essayistic restraint. Lead with the framework name and the precedent it builds on.
- **Sidebar.io:** Keep design-practice relevance in the first sentence of paragraph 2. Sacha's readers skip operator/growth content without a craft hook.
- **Not Boring:** DM-only. Frame as "this changed how I'd frame X" where X is Packy's own ongoing thesis.
- **Smashing / A List Apart:** Pitch-first; do not attach the full essay. Describe the argument in 60–80 words and offer to send or republish.
- **UX Collective:** Submit via Medium publication flow, not email. Keep canonical tag pointing to briantighe.design.
- **Pragmatic Engineer / Stratechery / Benedict Evans:** No email pitch. Earn citation via public engagement with substantive commentary on their work over weeks or months.

## What to omit

- Social-proof padding ("this essay has been shared by...")
- Self-commentary on the essay's reception
- Framing as a "submission for your newsletter" — let the ask speak plainly
- Enthusiasm performance ("so excited to share...")
- Apologies ("sorry to bother you")
- Meta-commentary on the pitch itself ("I don't usually do this but...")
