# LinkedIn Post Template

Apply this template when generating LinkedIn distribution artifacts. The structure reflects what performs on LinkedIn in 2026 for operator-researcher category positioning, not generic growth-hacker patterns.

## Structural template

```
[OPENING CLAIM — 1-2 sentences stating the counterintuitive observation or framework core]

[PRECEDENT — 1 sentence referencing a named historical pattern, operator, or research result]

[FRAMEWORK EXTRACTION — 3-5 sentences laying out the framework in operator-usable terms]
[One idea per line for algorithmic readability]
[Sentence-per-line formatting throughout this section]

[RECEIPT — 1-2 sentences grounding the framework in a specific quantified example]

[READER IMPLICATION — 1-2 sentences connecting to a decision the reader is likely facing]

[Full essay: briantighe.design/essays/[slug]]
```

## Character budget

- Target: 1,400–1,700 characters
- Hard ceiling: 1,800 characters
- Minimum viable: 1,100 characters

LinkedIn's 2026 algorithm prefers posts in the 1,400–1,700 range because they exceed the "see more" truncation threshold (forcing expansion to read) while remaining below the engagement-decay threshold (where readers abandon before completion).

## Formatting requirements

- **Sentence-per-line** within the framework extraction section — this is a LinkedIn-specific algorithmic pattern
- **Double line break between sections** for visual pacing
- **No hashtags** — violates voice markers and signals growth-hacker positioning
- **No emoji** — violates voice markers
- **Link on its own line** at the bottom, preceded by a blank line
- **No inline links** above the fold — LinkedIn's algorithm penalizes above-fold outbound links

## Example — known-good artifact

The following is a known-good LinkedIn post for the flagship essay "Prototype-Led Positioning." Study the structural pacing and voice before generating new artifacts.

---

Every positioning exercise in the twenty years before 2024 produced the same artifact: a deck. Fifteen slides, maybe twenty, laying out category, competitive frame, messaging hierarchy.

In 2026, that logic has inverted.

The inversion is enabled specifically by the 2024–2026 generation of AI-assisted prototyping tools — Claude Code, Cursor, Figma Make, v0.

Before these tools, the cost of a working prototype exceeded the cost of a deck by roughly 100×.

After them, the two costs have converged.

This is Prototype-Led Positioning — the practice of treating a working, shippable prototype as the primary positioning artifact.

The deck, if it exists at all, becomes a derivative summary.

At HelloFresh, a Figma Make prototype tested in 11 days produced a +$75M conversion lift — an experiment that would have required six weeks of engineering work under the deck-first operating model.

The implication for product leaders reading this: your next strategy document is worth less than your next shippable experiment.

Consider what positioning work you have queued for Q2 that could be replaced with a prototype your team ships next week.

## Generation rules specific to LinkedIn

1. **Lead with the framework, not the receipt**. LinkedIn's audience — VPs, directors, senior ICs in product and design — responds to framework-first structure. Receipt supports the framework rather than opening the post.

2. **One framework per post**. If the essay introduces multiple frameworks, pick the most central one for the LinkedIn artifact. Other frameworks can be teased in editorial notes as candidates for future separate posts.

3. **Name at least one specific precedent** (person, company, historical pattern). Posts without grounded references read as thought-leadership-in-a-vacuum. Avoid naming the same precedent in consecutive posts.

4. **The reader implication must be specific to a decision**, not generic. "Consider what positioning work you have queued for Q2 that could be replaced with a prototype your team ships next week" is specific. "This should change how you think about strategy" is generic and prohibited.

5. **The essay link is the only CTA**. Do not include "subscribe," "follow me," "let me know your thoughts," or any other secondary CTA. The essay link does the work.

## What to avoid

Do not write LinkedIn posts that:

- Open with a question ("Ever wondered why...?")
- Include the phrase "hot take," "unpopular opinion," or "contrarian view"
- Use numbered lists of 5+ items as the primary structure
- End with "what do you think?" or similar engagement-fishing
- Reference the author's own credentials ("As a Principal Designer at Yahoo Mail...")
- Include "↓" or "👇" or any other directional pointer to comments or links

## Voice check before finalizing

Apply the test: would Julie Zhuo's Looking Glass reader, scrolling LinkedIn, stop on this post because the framing is analytically serious? If the post reads as growth-hacker cadence or generic thought-leadership, rewrite.

The register is closer to a Julie Zhuo, Scott Belsky, or Ben Thompson LinkedIn appearance than to any standard LinkedIn thought-leadership account. Error toward seriousness.
