# Voice Markers — Brian Tighe

These are the calibration rules for every artifact generated by the distribution skill. Violations of these markers are more harmful than insufficient cleverness. Preserve markers even when it produces slightly less punchy copy.

## Lexical markers

### Words to use deliberately

- **operator** — preferred over "founder," "executive," or "professional" when referring to senior individuals in product, design, or growth roles
- **receipt** — concrete evidence grounded in specific metric or case study (e.g., "the HelloFresh receipt is +$75M in conversion value")
- **substrate** and **synthesis** — paired concepts referring to research material (substrate) and editorial output (synthesis)
- **compound** and **compounding** — use in financial-investment connotation, never in diluted growth-hacking sense
- **apparatus** — used specifically to refer to the briantighe.design research program as a whole, not as generic synonym for "system"
- **cadence** — preferred over "frequency" when discussing publishing rhythm
- **load-bearing** — used specifically for components that cannot be removed without structural failure

### Words and phrases to avoid

- "game-changer," "revolutionary," "disruption" (and any disruption-ism)
- "10x," "game-changing," "next-level," "unlock" as verb
- "hack," "hacks," "hacked" in content-marketing sense
- "content creator" — Brian is an operator-researcher, not a creator
- "personal brand" — Brian has a research program, not a brand
- "audience" as primary object of work — the work is primary, the audience is downstream
- Any language resembling Gary Vaynerchuk, growth-hacker Twitter, or LinkedIn thought-leadership parody

### Hashtags and emoji

- Never use hashtags in LinkedIn posts
- Never use emoji in any distribution artifact
- Exception: em-dashes and en-dashes are encouraged where they improve reading rhythm

## Structural markers

### Opening patterns

- Open with a specific claim or observation, not a question
- First sentence should be declarative and contain at least one concrete noun (not abstraction alone)
- Avoid opening with "I" — the work is primary, not the author

### Sentence construction

- Use em-dashes rather than semicolons for mid-sentence pivots
- Include at least one named precedent or historical reference per substantive argument when space permits
- Prefer complex sentences with clear clause structure over short choppy ones
- Periodic sentences (building to their point) are welcomed in LinkedIn posts

### Closing patterns

- Close with a specific implication for the reader's decision-making or work
- Avoid generic calls-to-action ("let me know what you think," "share your thoughts")
- If the essay links to briantighe.design, the link should appear on its own line after a blank line, never inline in the final paragraph

## Tonal markers

- Analytically rigorous without being academic
- Opinionated without being declarative-arrogant — state positions with appropriate hedging when warranted
- Comfortable with complexity; resist oversimplification
- Never perform confidence — state things plainly with hedges where genuine uncertainty exists
- Reference precedent to ground arguments rather than invent novel claims without support

## Framework naming

When referring to Brian's proprietary frameworks, use the exact capitalized form declared in `_data/frameworks.js`:

- "Prototype-Led Positioning" (not "prototype-led positioning" or "PLP")
- "Experiment Interfaces" (not "experiment interfaces" or "EI")
- "Agent-Mediated UX" (not "agent-mediated UX" or "AMUX")

First mention of a framework in any artifact should include the full name. Subsequent references in the same artifact may use a shortened form if natural, but prefer repeating the full name for clarity.

## Category positioning

Brian's work sits in the "design engineering for growth" category. Distribution artifacts should reinforce this category positioning without explicitly announcing it in every post. The category is demonstrated through what the framework discusses, not through category-labeling language.

Do not use the phrases "thought leadership," "personal brand," "content strategy," or "category creation" in any artifact. These phrases signal the wrong operating lineage (operator-newsletter rather than longitudinal researcher).

## Test for voice drift

Before finalizing any artifact, apply this test: would Julie Zhuo, reading this post, recognize it as belonging to the same editorial register as her own Looking Glass posts? If not — if the post sounds more like Gary Vaynerchuk, Tim Ferriss, or a generic LinkedIn thought-leadership account — rewrite until it passes this test.

The register is closer to Ben Thompson, Julie Zhuo, John Maeda, Christopher Alexander, and Edward Tufte than to any LinkedIn growth-hacker archetype. Error toward analytical seriousness.
